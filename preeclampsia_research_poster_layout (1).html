Background & motivation
Preeclampsia affects ~5–8% of pregnancies globally and is a leading cause of maternal mortality. Black women face 2–3× higher incidence yet are more frequently diagnosed late — a documented disparity in both clinical practice and predictive tools.

Research questions
RQ1 — Can an XGBoost model accurately predict preeclampsia risk from first-trimester clinical predictors?
RQ2 — Does model performance vary significantly across ethnic subgroups?
RQ3 — Can SHAP provide clinically interpretable per-patient explanations?

Conclusions
XGBoost achieves strong discrimination (AUC 0.87) on first-trimester predictors.
Significant performance gap exists for Black patients — bias auditing is essential.
SHAP provides actionable, per-patient explanations aligned with clinical knowledge.
Limitations & future work
Synthetic data limits generalisability — real EMR validation needed. Single-centre design. Future: prospective validation, fairness-aware retraining, clinician usability study.
